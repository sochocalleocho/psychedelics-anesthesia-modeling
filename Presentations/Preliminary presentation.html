Experimental Findings

  • DOI (psychedelic) actively reverses anesthesia induced by both Propofol and Isoflurane.
    • Note: Isoflurane has a broad mechanism of action, affecting GABAA, Glycine, and K+ channels.
  • Lisuride (non-psychedelic) FAILS to reverse anesthesia, despite being a high-affinity agonist at 5-HT2A.
  • Conclusion: Modeling the recovery of wakefulness based solely on 5-HT2A affinity is insufficient. The model must account for more specific functional selectivity that distinguishes psychedelic from non-psychedelic pathways.

The Molecular Difference

Why is DOI psychedelic while Lisuride is not, even though both bind 5-HT2A?

Biased Agonism (Functional Selectivity)

1. DOI (Psychedelic)
Recruits Gq/11 protein pathway.
→ Phospholipase C → Closing of K+ channels.
2. Lisuride (Non-Hallucinogenic)

→ Despite binding, it fails to close K+ channels ("The Agonist Paradox").

Micro Scale: AdEx Neuron Model

1. Voltage Dynamics ($V$)

$$C_m \frac{dv}{dt} = -g_L(v-E_L) + g_L \Delta_T e^{\frac{v-v_T}{\Delta_T}} - w + I_{syn}$$
$g_L (30\text{nS})$: Leak. Pulls $V$ to $E_L (-70.6\text{mV})$.
$\Delta_T (2\text{mV})$: Slope Factor. Once $V > V_T$, exponential growth leads to Spike!
$-w$: Adaptation. Negative feedback current.
Reset: When $v > V_{peak}$, $v \leftarrow V_{reset}$ instantly.

2. Adaptation Dynamics ($w$)

$$\tau_w \frac{dw}{dt} = a(v-E_L) - w + b \sum \delta(t-t_{k})$$
$a$ (4nS): Gain (Level Setting). constant drag ($g_L+a$) that lowers overall firing rate.
$b$ (80pA): Fatigue (Adaptation). spike-triggered jump ($w \leftarrow w+b$) causes progressive slowing.
$\tau_w$ (144ms): Recovery. Timescale for fatigue to fade.

3. Pharmacological Modulation

DOI (Psychedelic): $\downarrow g_L$. Higher resistance ($1/g_L$) → Increased Excitability.
Propofol (Anesthetic): $\uparrow \tau_{syn}$ (in $I_{syn}$). Slower GABA decay → Prolonged Inhibition.

Micro to Meso: The Transfer Function

1. The Stochastic Input

We do not use a static current. We inject Poisson Spike Trains.

Poisson Process: Spike times are random, but average rate is fixed ($\nu_E, \nu_I$). Mimics "synaptic noise".

2. The Output Map

We measure Stationary Output Rate $\nu_{out}$ for every pair $(\nu_E, \nu_I)$.

3. The Analytical Fit

$$F = \frac{1}{2\tau_V} \cdot \text{erfc}\left( \frac{V_{thr}^{eff} - \mu_V}{\sqrt{2}\sigma_V} \right)$$ $$\mu_V(\dots, w) = \mu_{syn} + E_L - \frac{w}{g_L}$$
erfc: Complementary Error Function. Prob. that noise ($\sigma_V$) crosses threshold.

Meso Scale: The Mean-Field System

$$T \frac{d\nu_E}{dt} = F_E(\nu_E, \nu_I, W) - \nu_E$$ $$T \frac{d\nu_I}{dt} = F_I(\nu_E, \nu_I, W) - \nu_I$$
d/dt: Rate of change of population firing.
F(...): Target Rate. Driven by inputs ($v_E, v_I$) and noise.
$$\frac{dW}{dt} = -\frac{W}{\tau_w} + b\nu_E + \frac{a}{\tau_w}(\mu_V - E_L)$$
a(v-E_L): Sub-threshold Coupling. Voltage influences adaptation even without spiking. Larger $a$ dampens resonance.
-W/tau: Recovery. Adaptation fades away.
+b*v_E: Accumulation. Fatigue builds up with firing.

Dynamics: The "Tug-of-War"

Competing Shifts in $F$

The brain state is determined by where the Transfer Function curve intersects the Identity Line (Input = Output). These intersections are the "Fixed Points".

  • Anesthesia Action: Increases $\tau_{syn}^I$.
    This flattens the curve (drags it down).
    → The high-activity fixed point disappears. System falls to coma (low rate).
  • Psychedelic Action: Decreases $g_L$ (leak).
    This boosts the curve (pulls it up).
    → The high-activity fixed point (Red Dot) is restored.
